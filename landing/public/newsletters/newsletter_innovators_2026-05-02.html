Identifying Interactions at Scale for LLMs (7 minute read)

Researchers develop scalable algorithms SPEX and ProxySPEX to identify influential interactions in complex AI models like LLMs, enabling more transparent and trustworthy AI.

Why this matters: Enables researchers to understand and improve the safety and reliability of state-of-the-art AI systems powering many real-world applications.

The Berkeley Artificial Intelligence Research Blog

Gradient-based Planning for World Models at Longer Horizons (14 minute read)

GRASP is a new gradient-based planner for learned dynamics models that enables robust long-horizon planning by lifting the trajectory into virtual states and adding stochasticity for exploration.

Why this matters: This allows researchers to leverage powerful world models for control, planning, and decision-making in high-dimensional, long-horizon tasks previously thought intractable.

The Berkeley Artificial Intelligence Research Blog

RL without TD learning (9 minute read)

A new RL algorithm based on divide-and-conquer, not temporal difference learning, scales well to long-horizon tasks, addressing key limitations of existing off-policy RL methods.

Why this matters: This opens new paths to scaling off-policy RL for complex, long-horizon problems in robotics, dialogue, healthcare, and other high-stakes domains.

The Berkeley Artificial Intelligence Research Blog

Whole-Body Conditioned Egocentric Video Prediction (7 minute read)

A model that predicts egocentric video from human actions, enabling simulation of physical interactions and long-term video generation from embodied agents.

Why this matters: This opens new possibilities for AI agents to learn and plan through embodied, first-person interaction with the physical world.

The Berkeley Artificial Intelligence Research Blog

What exactly does word2vec learn? (6 minute read)

This paper provides a quantitative theory for how word2vec learns word embeddings, showing it reduces to unweighted least-squares matrix factorization with a closed-form solution.

Why this matters: This sheds light on the linear structure of word embeddings, enabling better understanding and control of language model representations.

The Berkeley Artificial Intelligence Research Blog
